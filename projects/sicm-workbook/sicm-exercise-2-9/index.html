Exercise 2.9: Euler Angles

It is not immediately obvious that all orientations can be represented in terms of the Euler angles. To show that the Euler angles are adequate to represent all orientations, solve for the Euler angles that give an arbitrary rotation $R$. Keep in mind that some orientations do not correspond to a unique representation in terms of Euler angles.

We will solve this for the “3-1-3” euler angles which involves an initial rotation around the $z$ axis, followed by the $x$ axis and then the $z$ axis again.

From Section 2.7, we know that rotations about $z$ and $x$ axes can be represented as:

$$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{R}_z(\psi) &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\psi & -\sin\psi & 0\\ \sin\psi & \cos\psi & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \mathbf{R}_x(\psi) &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & \cos\psi & -\sin\psi\\ 0 & \sin\psi & \cos\psi\\ \end{bmatrix}\\ \end{align*} $$

The rotation $R$ can then be represented in matrix form as:

$$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{R}(\theta, \varphi, \psi) = \mathbf{R}_z(\varphi) \mathbf{R}_x(\theta) \mathbf{R}_z(\psi) \end{align*} $$

Expanding this matrix multiplication, we can get the matrix $\mathbf{R}$ as a function of $(\theta, \varphi, \psi)$

t = symbols('t', real=True)
theta, phi, psi = symbols('theta phi psi', real=True)

Rz = Matrix([[cos(t), -sin(t), 0],
             [sin(t), cos(t), 0],
             [0, 0, 1]])

Rx = Matrix([[1, 0, 0],
             [0, cos(t), -sin(t)],
             [0, sin(t), cos(t)]])

R = Rz.subs(t, phi) * Rx.subs(t, theta) * Rz.subs(t, psi)
R
$\displaystyle \left[\begin{matrix}- \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)} + \cos{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} & - \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)} - \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\phi \right)} & \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \sin{\left(\theta \right)}\\\sin{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} + \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)} & - \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \sin{\left(\psi \right)} + \cos{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)} & - \sin{\left(\theta \right)} \cos{\left(\phi \right)}\\\sin{\left(\psi \right)} \sin{\left(\theta \right)} & \sin{\left(\theta \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} & \cos{\left(\theta \right)}\end{matrix}\right]$

The columns of $\mathbf{R}$ represent the orientations of the coordinate axes post-rotation. That is, if $\hat{x}$ becomes $\hat{x}’$ after being rotated by $R$, then

$$ \hat{x}' = \begin{bmatrix}- \sin{(\phi )} \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\theta )} + \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )}\\\sin{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )} + \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\theta )}\\\sin{(\psi )} \sin{(\theta )}\end{bmatrix} $$

Note that the components of $\hat{x}’$ are being represented in terms of the original un-rotated frame.

By Euler’s rotation theorem, any arbitrary rotation can be represented by an axis $\vec{v}$ and an angle $\delta$.

If the coordinate axis $\hat{x}$ gets rotated into $\hat{x}‘$ by $R$, then $\hat{x} \cdot \hat{x}’ = \cos\delta$. Therefore,

$$ \begin{align*} \cos\delta = \hat{x}' \cdot \hat{x} &= \begin{bmatrix}- \sin{(\phi )} \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\theta )} + \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )}\\ \sin{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )} + \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\theta )}\\ \sin{(\psi )} \sin{(\theta )}\end{bmatrix} \cdot [1, 0, 0] = - \sin{(\phi )} \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\theta )} + \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )} \\ \\ \implies \delta &= \cos^{-1}{\left(- \sin{(\phi )} \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\theta )} + \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )}\right)} \end{align*} $$

The axis of rotation, $\vec{v}$ is perpendicular to the initial and final orientations of any vector undergoing rotation, and is therefore equal to the cross-product the pre- and post-rotation vectors.

$$ \begin{align*} \vec{v} &= \hat{x} \times \hat{x}'\\ &= [1, 0, 0] \times \begin{bmatrix}- \sin{(\phi )} \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\theta )} + \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )}\\ \sin{(\phi )} \cos{(\psi )} + \sin{(\psi )} \cos{(\phi )} \cos{(\theta )}\\ \sin{(\psi )} \sin{(\theta )}\end{bmatrix}\\ &= \left[\begin{matrix}0 & - \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \sin{\left(\theta \right)} & \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} + \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)}\end{matrix}\right] \end{align*} $$ 
xhat = R[:,0]
x = Matrix([[1, 0, 0]])

v = x.cross(xhat)

$\displaystyle \left[\begin{matrix}0 & - \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \sin{\left(\theta \right)} & \sin{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\psi \right)} + \sin{\left(\psi \right)} \cos{\left(\phi \right)} \cos{\left(\theta \right)}\end{matrix}\right]$

We have shown above that any arbitrary rotation, as represented by an axis and an angle, can be written in terms of Euler angles. This proves that Euler angles can represent any arbitrary rotation.
